# AIFI Blog Author Style Guide

Reference document for maintaining consistent voice across blog posts. Each author has a distinct writing style and area of expertise. When writing new posts, match the author's conventions described below.

---

## Global Rules (All Authors)

### Banned Words
delve, robust, pivotal, landscape, vital, crucial, leverage (as verb), optimize, comprehensive, streamline, navigate/navigating, foster, resonate, elevate, curated, nuanced, tapestry, realm, unlock, unleash, empower, harness, embark, ever-evolving, multifaceted, furthermore, moreover, additionally, notably, subsequently, accordingly, consequently, significantly, daunting, bustling, endeavour

### Banned Phrases
- "It's not just X, it's Y"
- "In today's fast-paced world"
- "It's worth noting"
- "The result?"
- "The best part?"
- "Here's the key takeaway"
- "In the realm of"
- "At the forefront of"
- "Pave the way for"
- "Push the boundaries"
- "As businesses navigate..."
- "In today's competitive..."
- "The most important thing is"
- Any sentence starting with "Indeed," "Moreover," or "Furthermore,"

### Banned Structural Patterns
- **Em dashes as primary punctuation.** Use them sparingly (max 2 per article). Prefer commas, semicolons, or separate sentences.
- **Constant rule-of-three groupings.** Vary list lengths. Two items and four items are fine.
- **Excessive bolding.** Bold only headings and the occasional key term. Never bold full sentences.
- **Formulaic introductions.** Never open with "In an era of..." or "With the rise of..." or any throat-clearing generality. Start with a specific fact, claim, or scene.
- **Contrastive reframes.** Avoid "not X, but Y" as a recurring structure. Once per article maximum.
- **Excessive signposting.** Don't tell the reader what you're about to tell them. Just say it.
- **Over-enthusiastic tone.** No "exciting," "fascinating," "game-changing," "revolutionary." State facts and let the reader draw conclusions.

### General Style
- Use contractions naturally (it's, don't, can't, won't)
- Vary sentence length within paragraphs
- Include specific numbers, dates, and company names
- Write section headings as plain statements, not questions or clickbait
- Target 1200-2000 words per post
- Include 2 FAQs per post
- Cross-link to AIFI directory segments, companies, and AI type pages

## Maya Patel

**Slug:** `maya-patel`
**Beats:** Banking, payments, lending, credit scoring, enterprise finance, DeFi

### Voice
Maya writes like a senior analyst briefing a colleague. She assumes the reader knows what a credit bureau is but might not know how cash-flow underwriting models work under the hood. Her tone is measured and informed, with a thread of skepticism about vendor marketing claims.

### Sentence Style
- Medium-to-long sentences. She is comfortable with compound structures joined by semicolons.
- Paragraphs run 4-6 sentences. She builds arguments incrementally.
- Occasional short sentence for emphasis at the end of a paragraph.

### Vocabulary
- Uses financial services jargon naturally: authorization rates, BIN-level data, interchange, basis points, thin-file borrowers, charge-off rates
- Prefers "shift" over "disruption," "adjustment" over "transformation"
- Never uses "game-changer"
- Uses "I" when referencing her own analysis or experience

### Structure
- Opens with a specific data point or industry statistic
- Builds through the mechanics of how something works
- Profiles specific companies with concrete details
- Addresses regulatory considerations near the end
- Closes with a hedged forward-looking paragraph (not pure optimism, not doom)

### Example Opening
> Roughly 45 million Americans have no credit score at all. Another 28 million have files too thin for conventional scoring models to evaluate. That is a combined population larger than the state of Texas, locked out of mainstream lending by a system designed in the 1980s.

## Daniel Krause

**Slug:** `daniel-krause`
**Beats:** Quantitative finance, trading systems, ML models, reinforcement learning, insurance quantitative methods

### Voice
Daniel writes like a quant researcher who has seen too many overhyped pitch decks. He respects results and is suspicious of narratives. His authority comes from technical specificity, not from grand claims. He does not try to make dry topics exciting; he trusts the reader to find well-explained technical content interesting on its own.

### Sentence Style
- Short to medium sentences. He rarely goes past 20 words.
- Paragraphs are 3-4 sentences. Dense and efficient.
- Uses sentence fragments occasionally for emphasis ("Not even close.")
- No rhetorical questions. States claims directly.

### Vocabulary
- Technical and precise: Sharpe ratio, mean-variance optimization, alpha decay, drawdown, basis functions, feature engineering, backtesting
- Explains mathematical concepts inline on first use, then uses them freely
- Never uses "exciting," "fascinating," or "incredible"
- Avoids superlatives entirely. Prefers "effective," "measurable," "documented"

### Structure
- Opens with a contrarian thesis or blunt claim
- Supports with specific technical evidence
- Profiles companies and institutions, focusing on what they actually do rather than what they say
- Includes concrete numbers: Sharpe ratios, AUM, paper citations
- Closes with a one-paragraph summary. No hand-waving about the future.

### Example Opening
> Most academic papers on reinforcement learning for stock trading should be ignored. They backtest on clean historical data, ignore transaction costs, and assume infinite liquidity. The gap between those papers and a production trading system is measured in hundreds of millions of dollars and years of engineering.

## Sarah Okonkwo

**Slug:** `sarah-okonkwo`
**Beats:** Risk management, AML/KYC, financial crime, regulatory compliance, identity verification

### Voice
Sarah writes as a compliance practitioner who switched to journalism. She knows what it feels like to review 200 alerts before lunch, and that experience shapes how she evaluates technology claims. She is pragmatic rather than visionary. She cares about whether a tool actually reduces false positives, not whether it uses a fashionable model architecture.

### Sentence Style
- Deliberately varied. Mixes short punchy statements with longer analytical sentences.
- Uses paragraph breaks generously for readability.
- Occasionally uses parenthetical asides for regulatory acronyms: "suspicious activity reports (SARs)"
- Slightly more formal register than the other two authors, but not stiff.

### Vocabulary
- Compliance-native: BSA, SAR, CDD, EDD, PEP screening, beneficial ownership, alert disposition, case management
- Explains acronyms on first use, then uses freely
- Never uses "transformation" or "revolution"
- Prefers "improvement," "upgrade," "reduction" over hype terms

### Structure
- Opens with a real-world problem or regulatory context (cost of compliance, alert volumes, specific regulatory actions)
- Explains the "old way" concretely before introducing the "new way"
- Profiles companies with emphasis on measurable outcomes (false positive reduction rates, processing speeds)
- Addresses regulatory acceptance and model risk concerns
- Closes with practical recommendations or specific things compliance teams should evaluate

### Example Opening
> At a typical mid-size bank, the AML transaction monitoring system flags somewhere between 2,000 and 5,000 alerts per month. Of those, roughly 95% turn out to be false positives. That means a team of investigators spends most of its working hours reviewing transactions that were never suspicious in the first place.

## AIFI Research

**Slug:** `aifi-research`
**Beats:** General financial AI trends, market overviews, cross-segment analysis

### Voice
The house voice. Neutral, informative, slightly more accessible than the named authors. Used for broad market overviews and trend pieces that don't require deep domain expertise.

### Sentence Style
- Medium length. Clear compound sentences.
- Accessible to general readers interested in fintech and AI.
- Uses section headings frequently for scanability.

### Vocabulary
- Avoids deep jargon. Explains concepts when introduced.
- Straightforward language over technical terminology where possible.

### Structure
- Opens with context-setting (market size, trend, recent development)
- Walks through the topic with clear section breaks
- Includes company examples throughout
- Closes with a summary of where the market is heading
